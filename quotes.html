Orbital  ·  Key Quotes

Quotes to Know

Organised by theme. Each quote is followed by a short analytical note — what technique is at work and why it matters.

How to Use This Page

Don't memorise quotes in isolation. For each one, know: what technique Harvey is using; what idea or theme it serves; and what effect it has on the reader. A single well-used quote with precise analysis will always outperform a long quotation with thin commentary.

✦

Earth & The View

"The earth from here, is like heaven."

p. 9

The simile inverts expectation — heaven is typically above, but from orbit the planet below assumes a sacred quality. Harvey positions Earth not as ordinary or taken for granted, but as something luminous and unreachable: the thing the astronauts long for. The inversion of perspective is the novel's central move.

"The earth from here is the face of an exulted lover … a mother waiting for her children to return."

p. 3

Double personification: Earth is simultaneously a lover and a mother. Both figures imply yearning and the pain of separation. Harvey's anthropomorphising of the planet is an ethical argument — if Earth is a living being capable of waiting, capable of being loved, then human damage to it becomes a form of betrayal. This is one of the novel's most explicit environmental positions.

"The astronauts watch it sleep and wake."

p. 3

A deceptively simple sentence that personifies Earth and positions the astronauts as quiet observers of something alive. The domestic verb "sleep and wake" domesticates the cosmic — making the planet feel intimate rather than vast. It also reverses the usual subject–object relationship: Earth is watched, not watching.

"A rolling indivisible globe which knows no possibility of separation."

p. 72

Harvey's most direct statement about the borderlessness of Earth from orbit. "Indivisible" is a political word — it denies the reality of national borders. "Knows no possibility of separation" is almost legal language, as if making a ruling. Harvey is using the vantage point of space to mount a quiet argument against nationalist division.

"Continents and countries come one after the other and the earth feels – not small, but almost endlessly connected."

p. 124

The correction ("not small, but") is important: Harvey resists the easy reading that seeing Earth from space makes it seem insignificant. Instead it reveals interconnection. The dash and qualifier slow the reader down — mimicking the act of looking carefully and revising one's first impression.

"The theatrical splendour of a hanging, spinning planet."

p. 54

"Theatrical" implies performance — as if Earth is putting on a show. But "hanging" gives it a vulnerable quality: suspended, precarious. "Spinning" is relentless, impersonal. Harvey holds sublimity and fragility in the same phrase, refusing to let either cancel the other out.

Time & Impermanence

"The past comes, the future, the past, the future. It's always now, it's never now."

p. 76

The alternating rhythm of the first sentence enacts the experience it describes — the reader feels the oscillation of sixteen sunrises. The paradox of the second sentence — "always now" and "never now" — captures the temporal dislocation of orbit: time both collapses and stretches. This is one of Harvey's most quoted lines and is essential for essays on time and structure.

"There could be no end, there can be only circles."

p. 14

A statement of orbital logic that Harvey extends into a structural and philosophical principle. The novel itself is circular — it does not resolve. This line alerts readers early that Harvey is not interested in endings: only in the ongoing return. It also gestures toward mortality: without end, there is only process.

"Old thoughts born into new moments."

pp. 8–9

The oxymoron "old thoughts / new moments" defines Harvey's narrative method: the novel moves between memory and the present, the personal past and the immediate experience of orbit. It also reflects what the crew does: they bring their entire previous lives into a context that renders those lives strange.

"We're windblown leaves. We think we're the wind, but we're just the leaf."

p. 106 (Shaun)

Shaun's most philosophical moment. The extended metaphor exposes the illusion of human agency: we believe we drive events (we are the wind) but we are in fact driven by larger forces (we are the leaf). It is also a quietly religious idea — consistent with Shaun's faith — that humans are subject to something beyond their control. Harvey uses the most grounded of images (wind, leaves) to make a cosmic point.

"In the cosmic calendar as it would exist then, all humans ever did and were will be a brief light that flickers on and off again one single day in the middle of the year, remembered by nothing."

p. 114

Harvey invokes Carl Sagan's Cosmic Calendar to dwarf the human story. "Remembered by nothing" is devastating — not even by indifference, but by absolute absence. The syntactic build-up of the sentence delays this final phrase, so the reader arrives at "nothing" with accumulated weight. Harvey is not despairing here — she is insisting that we see our scale accurately.

Connection & Shared Humanity

"We are one. … We can't be divided, this is the truth. We won't because we can't be. We drink each other's recycled urine. We breathe each other's recycled air."

p. 64

Harvey's most visceral statement of interdependence. The philosophical claim ("We are one") is immediately grounded in biology and necessity. The repetition of "recycled" and the parallel structure of the final two sentences has an almost liturgical quality — like a creed being recited. The deliberate shock of "urine" refuses sentimentality: connection is not romantic but material and inescapable.

"They are each to the other a representative of the human race – they each have to suffice for billions of people."

p. 18

"Suffice" is the key word — it implies effort, inadequacy, and necessity. Each crew member must stand in for everything the others have left behind: family, culture, landscape, language. The enormity of this is held in a quiet, declarative sentence. Harvey is making an argument about what community means when stripped to essentials.

"They were all familiar with Winnie-the-Pooh in one form or another in their five different countries."

p. 47

The bathos is deliberate and precise. Harvey does not use grand symbols of shared culture — she uses a children's bear. The specificity makes the argument more convincing: beneath national and political difference, there are childhood things held in common. Harvey trusts the ordinary to do her philosophical work.

"Remember this, each of them thinks."

p. 41

A moment of collective consciousness — Harvey gives the same thought to all six crew members simultaneously as they watch the Northern Lights. The brief, urgent imperative ("Remember this") captures the human instinct to preserve overwhelming experience against time. Harvey then notes that this thought is shared, without any dialogue: they do not say it to each other. They simply think it together.

The Body in Space

"All beings are living in life-support machines commonly called bodies."

p. 20

Harvey collapses the distinction between the astronauts' technological life-support systems and the biological ones all humans rely on. The defamiliarising phrase "commonly called bodies" makes the familiar strange — as if "body" is a technical term Harvey is being precise about. The implication is that all life is as precarious and dependent as life on the ISS.

"Misshapen bags of fluid."

p. 27

Blunt, reductive, comic, and quietly horrifying. Harvey deflates human self-importance with a single phrase that describes what microgravity does to the body. It is also a reminder that the human body — extraordinary as it is — is fundamentally biological and vulnerable. The phrase undercuts any grandeur the space mission might project.

"Just a spacesuit and piece of rope and her slender life."

Text (Nell, spacewalk)

The list — "spacesuit," "piece of rope," "her slender life" — moves from equipment to existence. "Slender" is a careful adjective: it suggests both physical fragility and preciousness. The tricolon builds to the most important item, and that item is revealed to be the most vulnerable. Harvey makes mortality feel immediate and material.

"In orbit his sense of life is simpler and gentler and more forgiving … His thoughts don't avalanche like they used to. They come and they interest him for as long as they need to and then they go."

Text (Pietro)

Harvey uses Pietro's atrophying body as a metaphor for a kind of cognitive peace. The simile of thoughts that "avalanche" gives way to a quiet rhythm — the staccato of "they come and they interest him and then they go." Pietro's physical diminishment produces psychological clarity. Harvey implies that stripping life back to essentials may be, paradoxically, a form of fullness.

Environment, Progress & Human Conflict

"Wherever mankind goes it leaves some kind of destruction behind it."

p. 69

One of Harvey's most direct statements about human impact. "Wherever" is universal — there is no exception, no alternative history where this is not so. Harvey places this observation in the context of space itself: even orbit is now littered with debris. The scale of human destructiveness, seen from above, is the novel's most uncomfortable ecological argument.

"They are like fortune tellers, the crew. Fortune tellers who can see and tell the future but do nothing to change or stop it."

Text (typhoon)

The simile of the fortune teller is devastating: knowledge without power. The crew can see the typhoon building, can photograph it, can report latitudes — but cannot protect a single person from it. Harvey uses this to crystallise the novel's central irony: the overview effect gives perspective but not agency. Seeing clearly is not the same as being able to act.

"The atom bomb and … fake stars they're going to put into space in the shape of company logos, and the buildings they're going to print on the moon."

p. 51 (Pietro)

Pietro's list moves from the catastrophic to the absurd, and the juxtaposition is the point: both are symptoms of the same human impulse — to extend dominance, to mark territory, to leave a signature. Harvey uses Pietro's voice to satirise human ambition and its disregard for what it damages. The dark comedy of "company logos" in space sits alongside the atom bomb — equal products of progress.

"A person is not beautiful because they're good, they're beautiful because they're alive."

p. 51 (Pietro)

Pietro's most humane moment. The correction ("not because … but because") insists that beauty is not contingent on virtue — it is inherent in existence itself. This is a response to the ecological crisis: the planet is beautiful not because it has been good or deserving, but because it is alive. Harvey uses Pietro to argue for the intrinsic value of life, separate from any utilitarian measure.

"In the late smash-it-all-up teenage stage of self-harm and nihilism."

p. 30

Harvey figures humanity's relationship to the planet as adolescent self-destruction. The metaphor is mordant and precise: not adult malice, but the particular recklessness of a species that knows better and can't stop. The colloquial energy of "smash-it-all-up" contrasts sharply with the clinical "nihilism" — Harvey is simultaneously affectionate and despairing about her species.

Hope, Faith & Meaning

"A big-hearted fanciful spasm of hope."

p. 29

Harvey's description of space exploration — and by extension, the novel itself. "Spasm" is involuntary: hope, here, is not a reasoned position but a bodily response. "Fanciful" acknowledges its impracticality. But "big-hearted" redeems it. Harvey refuses to mock the impulse to look outward, even while acknowledging its irrationality and cost.

"In loneliness and curiosity and hope, humanity looks outward."

p. 29

The tricolon "loneliness and curiosity and hope" gives three equally weighted, equally valid reasons for space travel. "Loneliness" is unexpected first — Harvey will not let the noble motives stand without the human ones. Together, the three form a portrait of the species: alone, wondering, reaching. The sentence is quietly one of the most compassionate in the novel.

"Pick a single creature on this earth and its story will be the earth's story."

p. 107

Harvey's most compressed statement of ecological interconnection. The imperative "Pick" invites the reader to test the claim themselves. The equivalence of "its story" and "the earth's story" refuses the hierarchy that places humans above other species — any creature's story carries the full weight of the planet's. This is Harvey at her most radical.

"What we do to the Earth we do to ourselves."

Harvey, Booker Prize acceptance speech

Harvey's own words, spoken when accepting the Booker Prize — and often considered the novel's thesis statement. The parallel construction makes damage to the planet and damage to humanity identical, not analogous. There is no separation between environmental ethics and self-interest. This line belongs on any essay about ecological themes in the text.

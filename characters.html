Orbital  ·  Character Analysis

The Crew

Six human beings, suspended between Earth and the void. Each carries a private world inside a vessel the size of a football field.

A Note on Character in Orbital

Harvey's characters are not developed through action or conventional dialogue — there is no quoted speech in the novel. Instead, character is revealed through interior monologue, memory, observation, and the way each person relates to what they see through the windows below. Harvey is more interested in consciousness than biography.

Harvey herself gives us a key image for how to read each character: she describes the crew as different organs of the spaceship's body. "Anton… the spaceship's heart. Pietro its mind, Roman… its hands, Shaun its soul… Chie… its conscience, Nell… its breath."

Individual Profiles

🇷🇺 Russian  ·  Commander  ·  The Hands

Roman

Roman is the current commander of the ISS and has been here before — twice. He is the most experienced member of the crew: "dextrous and capable, able to fix anything, control the robotic arm with millimetre precision, wire the most complex circuit board." Harvey describes him as the spaceship's hands — the one who makes things work.

He has a son, who gave him a small felt moon that he brought as a mascot on the voyage — it bobs and spins throughout the novel as a talisman of Earth and family. Roman taught Nell and Shaun, both first-timers, to move in microgravity with the mantra: "slow is smooth and smooth is fast." He plays poker with Anton using disc magnets as chips.

Roman is also the crew member who articulates the philosophical strangeness of spacewalks most directly: "perhaps it was caused by untapped memories of being in the womb. That's what being floating in space feels like for me. Being not yet born."

🇷🇺 Russian  ·  Cosmonaut  ·  The Heart

Anton

Anton arrived three months before Roman, Nell, and Shaun — he is part of the original crew alongside Pietro and Chie. He shares the Russian module with Roman. Harvey describes him as the spaceship's heart: "quiet, and dry in his humour, sentimental, crying openly at films, at scenes outside the window."

Anton tends a small onboard garden of cabbages and dwarf wheat — one of the novel's most tender details. He has children, and there are moments where his longing for them surfaces sharply. Harvey writes of him waking briefly in the night with one "piercing feeling of childhood joy" at the thought of a spacecraft heading to the moon — a flash of innocence recovered and immediately lost.

Where Roman is competent and composed, Anton is emotional and open — his sentimentality and his quiet humour make him the crew member who keeps the warmth of human life alive on board.

🇺🇸 American  ·  Astronaut  ·  The Soul

Shaun

Harvey describes Shaun as the crew's soul — the one who convinces the others that they have souls at all. He is Christian, wearing a crucifix on a chain beneath his chin, and this faith is a source of quiet tension with Nell, the atheist. They share a scene where each wonders, but doesn't ask, how the other can hold their position.

Shaun was shaped by watching the Challenger disaster — as was Nell, in a different way. His daughter sends him a video of a goat jumping on a trampoline with no message except "ILY!" — one of the novel's most human, comic, and quietly moving moments. He floats above the others "like an angel," tossing hazelnuts into his open mouth.

Shaun arrived with Roman and Nell three months into the mission, a first-timer. His faith and his warmth make him the character through whom Harvey most directly explores the question of meaning: what does it signify to be here?

🇬🇧 British  ·  Astronaut  ·  The Breath

Nell

Nell is described as the spaceship's breath — she has eight-litre diving lungs from her background as a freediver. Harvey repeatedly connects her to water: she freedived before she came here, and now she thinks of space as analogous to diving. "Once you've been on a spacewalk, looking at space through a window is never the same. It's like looking through bars at an animal you once ran with."

She is a former research meteorologist, which gives her a trained eye for the weather systems she watches from above. Her inner life in the novel is shaped by two things: the Challenger disaster, which she watched as a child and which crystallised her drive to become an astronaut; and the long-ago death of her mother, whose absence surfaces in a vivid dream.

Nell is the crew's atheist, short-haired, puffy-cheeked from microgravity, "perpetually startled." She has a husband who sends her chocolate-coated honeycomb in the resupply craft. Her scientific background — and her capacity for grief, wonder, and honest doubt — make her one of Harvey's most complete presences in the novel.

🇮🇹 Italian  ·  Astronaut  ·  The Mind

Pietro

Harvey describes Pietro as the spaceship's mind. He has been on the ISS for almost six months — longer than Roman, Shaun, and Nell — and his relationship to the experience is deeper and stranger. He thinks of himself as becoming a tadpole: "all head and no body." His muscles have atrophied, his appetite has faded. "In orbit his sense of life is simpler and gentler and more forgiving, not that his thoughts are different but that his thoughts are fewer and more distinct. They don't avalanche like they used to."

Pietro is the crew member most obviously in love with Earth. He listens to Duke Ellington on the treadmill and in his mind he is in "the wild mint meadows of Emilia-Romagna." He and Nell did a spacewalk together to install a spectrometer that measures the radiance of the Earth — and Pietro thinks about this instrument every day, because what it is measuring is whether the planet is dimming.

He had an intense longing for his wife early in the mission. In its place, later, came a dream about Nell — which left him so embarrassed he could barely look at her at breakfast. The dream never repeated, and something in him went quiet.

🇯🇵 Japanese  ·  Astronaut  ·  The Conscience

Chie

Harvey describes Chie as the spaceship's conscience — "methodical, fair, wise, not-quite definable or pin-downable." She grows protein crystals, runs brain scans, and tends the mice. She keeps her resistance high on the bike, counting pedal cadence with her teeth clenched. Her ponytail always wafts upright above her head.

During the novel's single day, Chie's mother dies. She came to the galley one Friday evening, "her face colourless with shock," and said simply: "My mother has died." The novel's fifth chapter — set on a small Japanese island, a wooden house, an old woman dying on her step at dusk — is Chie's mother's story, told from a distance. "She has thought she would try to wait another month for her daughter to come back. But since when did death wait."

Chie cannot return. She cannot hold her mother's hand. She can only orbit. Harvey uses her grief to ask one of the novel's most devastating questions: what does distance mean when you can see the whole Earth and reach none of it?

Now some dreams come to Chie in which her mother is alive; they're spiked with relief and exultation. Beneath her Japan and East Asia crowd the forward view and if she were to wake and look out she'd see little or nothing of the typhoon. She'd see only a lovely planet shifting unfalteringly past the places of her childhood.

The Crew as Collective

One of Harvey's most important arguments is that these six individuals, for all their differences, become something larger than themselves. She writes: "They are each to the other a representative of the human race – they each have to suffice for billions of people."

And yet the crew is not a utopia. There are days of resentment: "Nell might look at Shaun some days and resent him for failing to be her husband. Anton will wake resentful because none of these people is his daughter or son or anyone or anything he loves." Harvey is honest about this. The community of the ISS is not idealistic — it is necessary. They are all each other has.

The novel moves between the individual and the collective without warning. Harvey uses a collective "they" as well as individual perspectives, and the second-person "you" that puts the reader inside the experience. The crew's boundaries are permeable. At one point she writes that they even "dream the same dreams." By the end of the novel, individuality and collective humanity have become hard to separate — which is precisely Harvey's point.

The Ship's Body — Harvey's Key Metaphor

Anton – quiet, and dry in his humour, sentimental, crying openly at films, at scenes outside the window – Anton the spaceship's heart. Pietro its mind, Roman (the current commander, dextrous and capable, able to fix anything, control the robotic arm with millimetre precision, wire the most complex circuit board) its hands, Shaun its soul (Shaun there to convince them all that they have souls), Chie (methodical, fair, wise, not-quite definable or pin-downable) its conscience, Nell (with her eight-litre diving lungs) its breath.

Orbital — Orbit 4, descending

This extended metaphor is one of Harvey's most structurally important moves. By making the crew into organs of a single body, she argues for their interdependence — and dissolves the national and cultural categories that ordinarily separate them. They are not Russian or American or Japanese on the ISS. They are heart, mind, hands, soul, conscience, breath.

Essay Angle — Character

When writing about character, avoid plot summary. The most effective analysis will focus on: how Harvey uses different characters to embody different responses to the same experience; the specific language she uses to render interior states; and the tension she creates between individual identity and collective humanity. The "spaceship's body" metaphor is an excellent entry point for any essay on character.

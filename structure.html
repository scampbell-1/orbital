Orbital  ·  Structure of the Text

How the Novel is Built

Harvey's structure is not incidental — it is argument. The way Orbital is shaped enacts its themes before a word of content is read.

Key Principle — VATE Inside Stories 2026

"To a large extent, the structure of Harvey's novel articulates its thematic concerns. The upended nature of time experienced by those who travel into low Earth orbit is reflected in the book's architecture, in which a day comes around every ninety minutes."

✦
Structural Feature 1

Unity of Time — One Day, 22 Chapters, 16 Orbits

The novel is set over a single Tuesday in early October — twenty-four hours in the lives of six astronauts. Harvey divides this day into twenty-two chapters, each corresponding to a stage of the sixteen orbits the ISS completes in a day. The architecture is borrowed from Aristotle's dramatic concept of the unity of time: action confined to a single time period.

The effect is compression. By tightening the temporal frame, Harvey amplifies every moment — the mundane becomes significant, and each observation carries weight. The structure simultaneously asks readers to consider the significance of individual moments and the inconsequential nature of the individual against geological time.

Paradoxically, this tight timeframe is constantly interrupted by memory, flashback, and reflection — the characters escape the present through their inner lives, even as the structure insists on the present moment's primacy.

Unity of time Compression 22 chapters 16 orbits
Structural Feature 2

Circular Structure — No End, Only Circles

The novel does not resolve. It ends where it effectively began — the station still orbiting, the Earth still turning, another cycle beginning. This is deliberate. Harvey signals her structural intention early:

"There could be no end, there can be only circles."

p. 14

The circular structure mirrors the physical reality of orbit — the station never arrives anywhere, it simply continues. But Harvey extends this into a philosophical argument: life, like the orbit, is not a linear progress toward a destination. It is a series of returns, revisions, and repetitions. The trajectory of any life, like the craft itself, is not linear.

For essay writing, this is important: students should not look for a traditional climax and resolution in Orbital, because Harvey has deliberately removed them. The structure is the argument.

Circularity No resolution Return Structure as theme
Structural Feature 3

Third-Person Omniscient Narration — The Panoramic View

The novel is narrated in third person, but Harvey is not channelling any single character's perspective. The narration drifts between crew members in a seamless, fluid motion — we move from inside Roman's consciousness to Chie's to Pietro's without chapter breaks or signposting. The narrative stance is detached and panoramic, mirroring the view from orbit itself.

This detachment is not coldness — it is the structural equivalent of the overview effect. Just as the astronauts see the whole Earth at once rather than any single country, the narration sees the whole crew rather than privileging any single consciousness. Harvey is arguing through form that no one perspective is more valid than another.

The fragmented nature of the narrative — drifting, associative, non-linear — can also be read as mirroring the nonlinear nature of ecological degradation: slow, cumulative, resistant to a single cause or moment.

Omniscient narrator Shifting perspectives Meditative tone Fragmented narrative
Structural Feature 4

Second-Person Intrusions — You Are Here

Harvey periodically shifts into the second person, addressing the reader directly as "you." These passages pull the reader out of the role of observer and into the experience itself. The most significant examples are the perspective of a Filipino fisherman awaiting the coming typhoon (pp. 38–39) and, more startlingly, the speculative viewpoints of aliens observing humanity from further out in space (p. 2 and p. 87).

These second-person intrusions do several things. They dissolve the boundary between the astronauts' experience and the reader's. They extend the novel's vantage point — not just from the ISS, but from the ground below, and from beyond the solar system. And they lend the novel a quality the VATE guide calls "surreal" — moments where realism gives way to something more speculative and philosophically vertiginous.

Second person Reader positioning Filipino fisherman Alien perspective Surreal element
Structural Feature 5

Flashback and Memory — Escape from the Present

Within the tight single-day frame, Harvey repeatedly moves backward in time through memory and flashback. Characters' personal histories surface as they orbit: Nell's childhood experience watching the Challenger disaster; Roman's first arrival on the ISS; Chie's recollection of her mother; Pietro's memory of his wife.

These flashbacks serve multiple structural purposes. They provide relief from the compressed present — a kind of temporal breathing room within the tight twenty-four hour structure. They also reveal that the crew's individual histories often mirror one another's, raising questions about the "artificial nature of division, both national and individual." The structural movement between present and past enacts what the novel argues thematically: that the past and present are not separate, but continuous and orbiting one another.

"Old thoughts born into new moments."

pp. 8–9

Flashback Memory Non-linear time Personal history
Structural Feature 6

Dualities — Held in Tension, Not Resolved

Harvey structures the novel's ideas as a series of dualities — pairs of opposites that are held in tension throughout but never fully resolved. The VATE guide identifies the central dualities as:

Earth

vs Space

Motion

vs Stillness

Isolation

vs Connection

Science

vs Emotion

Time

vs Timelessness

Significance

vs Insignificance

These dualities are not resolved by the novel's end — they are the condition of existence that Harvey is mapping. The reader is "asked to dwell in the often-incongruous knowledge that we are a species capable of invention and innovation but are also tremendously vulnerable. Further, we simultaneously yearn for connection whilst facilitating heartbreaking destruction." (VATE, p. 9)

Dualities Paradox Unresolved tension
Structural Feature 7

The ISS as Liminal Space

The 'orbiting laboratory' (p. 133) functions structurally as a liminal space — a threshold, a space between worlds. The crew members are neither on Earth nor in deep space; they are suspended in-between. This liminality shapes the novel's entire architecture: the characters are removed from the contexts that ordinarily define them (nation, culture, family, routine) and placed in a space where those definitions no longer hold.

Chie's grief is the clearest example of how the ISS functions as a structural cocoon. The VATE guide notes that "it's only when she goes back that she knows that her mother is dead" (p. 13) — meaning the full emotional reality of the loss cannot be processed in orbit. The ISS holds it in suspension. The liminal space defers reckoning.

Harvey also describes the ISS as a "Foucauldian heterotopia" — a real space that exists outside the domain of all national, cultural, and territorial boundaries, inverting dominant norms. It is a 'nationless, borderless outpost' (p. 63) that contests the logic of the world below.

Liminal space Heterotopia In-between Suspension
Structural Feature 8

Paradoxical Emotions — The Emotional Architecture

In their training, the astronauts are warned of the paradoxical emotions that will result from viewing Earth from a distance. Harvey names them directly:

"Exhilaration, anxiety, rapture, depression, tenderness, anger, hope, despair."

p. 73

This list — which holds opposites side by side without hierarchy — is itself a structural model for the novel. Harvey does not privilege one emotional register over another. Wonder and grief sit together. Hope and despair are equally present. The structure of the novel enacts this emotional simultaneity: it does not build toward a single feeling but holds many contradictory ones in orbit around one another.

Paradox Emotional simultaneity Overview effect No hierarchy of feeling

Essay Angle — Structure

The most sophisticated responses to Orbital will treat structure as inseparable from meaning. Ask: why does Harvey set the novel in one day? What does the circular structure argue about life? Why does she shift perspective rather than focussing on a single narrator? How do the second-person intrusions change the reader's relationship to the material? Structure in this novel is never neutral — every formal choice is a thematic statement.

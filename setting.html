Where the Novel Lives

Two settings exist in Orbital: the sealed interior of the ISS, and the whole of planet Earth below. Neither is backdrop — both are active presences in the novel.

The International Space Station

The ISS is the size of a football field but livable space is closer to that of a six-bedroom house. It is loud — filled with the constant hum of life-support systems — and extraordinarily cramped. Crew members sleep in sleeping bags tethered in small personal compartments. They exercise for hours each day to prevent bone and muscle loss.

Harvey renders this environment with precision. The ISS in Orbital is not a glamorous or heroic place — it is a workplace where people eat reconstituted food, manage maintenance schedules, and try to sleep while floating. This deliberate unglamorousness is important: Harvey wants to make the extraordinary ordinary, and the ordinary extraordinary.

Key Space

The Cupola

The cupola is a dome-shaped observation module with seven windows looking down at Earth. It is the novel's most significant physical space — the place where the crew goes to look, to think, to grieve, to wonder. Harvey returns to it repeatedly. It functions as a threshold: between the sealed interior and the void; between the human world and the planetary one; between doing and simply being.

Earth as Setting

Earth in Orbital is not a single place — it is every place, seen whole. Harvey describes specific geographies as the station passes over them: the Sahara, the Pacific, the Himalayas, storm systems over the Philippines, the lights of cities at night. Each pass brings a different face of the planet.

But Earth is also a setting the crew cannot enter. They can see it, name it, mourn it — but they cannot touch it. This inaccessibility is one of the novel's defining tensions. The most familiar place in existence becomes strange and unreachable.

Time as Setting

Harvey treats time itself as a kind of setting. On the ISS, time is dislocated: clocks run on UTC, divorced from any particular place's sunrise or sunset. Sixteen sunrises happen in twenty-four hours. The novel takes place in one day — but that day contains multitudes.

This temporal strangeness reinforces the novel's themes of impermanence and the inadequacy of human categories. Time, like national borders, looks different from 400 kilometres up.

Essay Angle — Setting

When writing about setting, avoid treating it as mere backdrop. In Orbital, setting is argument. The ISS's confinement and the Earth's inaccessibility are the conditions that make the novel's ideas possible. Ask: what does this setting make the characters think and feel that they could not think or feel anywhere else? How does Harvey use physical space to explore her themes?

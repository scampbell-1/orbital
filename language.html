Orbital  ·  Language & Style

How Harvey Writes

Harvey has articulated the 'sheen of strangeness and otherliness' she believed needed to be brought to the writing of a tale set in low Earth orbit. The way she writes is inseparable from what she is trying to say.

Why Craft Matters for VCE

In VCE English, analysis of language and style is not optional — it is the point. Understanding what Harvey is saying is necessary but not sufficient. You need to be able to explain how she achieves her effects: what specific choices she makes at the level of sentence, structure, imagery, and perspective, and why those choices matter. This page gives you the tools to do that.

Prose Style

Style

The 'Space Pastoral'

While a thoroughly contemporary novel reflecting current environmental threats, Harvey's lyrical style and meditative tone — in combination with the text's philosophical underpinnings — echo Romantic literature's pursuit of the sublime via connection with the natural world. The astronauts' orbit around Earth provides a panoramic perspective of the planet, and Harvey's poetic descriptions pay homage to the aching beauty, grandeur, terror and fragility bestowed by the Romantics onto the natural world.

The Earth is framed not just as scenery, but as an emotionally and metaphysically potent source with whose ecosystems humanity is firmly interconnected. The distance from which the crew view their planet facilitates the characters' reflection on what it means to feel in a world that so often demands emotional detachment. The characters reveal what it is to experience humble belonging to that which is far greater and of which we lack full comprehension, and to exist as a vulnerable and finite being on a planet that is both our cradle and crucible.

Romantic echoes The sublime Panoramic perspective Beauty and terror
Style

Sentence as Orbit

Like the planet she describes, the language of Harvey's brief novel can be characterised by its often elliptical 'Ceaseless movement' (p. 56), foreshadowed by the inclusion of Emma Lopes' map of 24 Hours of Earth Orbits with Daylight in the Northern Hemisphere which precedes Orbit minus 1. Sentences drift, loop and spiral like the orbiting craft — 'old thoughts born into new moments' (pp. 8–9).

In the absence of a classic plotline, the narrative arc is created and maintained by the rhythm and musicality of the language and the shift from sections devoted to mesmerising and wondrous imagery followed by decidedly domestic moments. Harvey's sentence rhythm shifts depending on what she is describing. Passages about the view from the cupola tend to use longer, more expansive sentences. Passages about the body — eating, exercising, experiencing grief — are often more clipped and direct. The rhythm does emotional work.

Elliptical movement Sentence rhythm Lyrical prose Pacing
Style

'Space-Realism' and Technical Detail

At times at odds with the subtly woven references to characters' interiority, Harvey's presentation of her novel as 'space-realism' is reflected in the degree of technical detail she provides, welcoming readers into each character's present subjectivity as well as the spacecraft itself.

Their hands are in sealed experiment boxes or assembling or disassembling ruggedised units or refilling the auto-release food pouches in the modules of mice, their feet are in tethers at their workstations, their screwdrivers and spanners and scissors and pencils are drifting here and there about their heads and shoulders, a pair of tweezers breaks loose and sails towards the air vents which, in their imperceptible sucking, are the resting place of all lost things

p. 37

This sense of realism and authenticity is reinforced using a range of technical terminology — specific equipment, scientific processes, the precise mechanics of life in microgravity. Harvey grounds the extraordinary in the mundane, making the reader feel the texture of daily life aboard the ISS before pulling back to the cosmic view.

Space-realism Technical terminology Authenticity Cosmic vs domestic

Key Techniques

Narration

The Second-Person Voice

One of Orbital's most striking formal choices is its use of second-person narration — passages addressed directly to 'you'. When Harvey writes 'you look down and see…', she pulls the reader into the experience. The boundary between the astronauts and the person reading dissolves.

The effect is multiple. It creates intimacy — the reader is placed inside the experience rather than observing it. It also creates an ethical responsibility: if you are looking down at the planet, then what you see is your concern too. The second person is how Harvey makes the overview effect personal to everyone who reads the novel.

The narration shifts across the novel — Harvey moves between third-person singular (focusing on individual characters), a collective third-person 'they', and the second-person 'you'. This fluid movement resists any single, fixed perspective, which is itself a thematic argument: there is no one stable vantage point, even from space.

Second person Reader positioning Intimacy Multiple perspectives
Form

The Absence of Dialogue

There is no directly quoted speech in Orbital. Characters do not speak to one another in any conventional sense — there are no speech marks, no exchanges, no conversations rendered on the page. This keeps the reader inside consciousness rather than in the social world. Without dialogue, the novel has an almost meditative quality — like a long held breath.

Rich in imagery and poetic prose, much of what readers learn about the characters is implied rather than directly stated. Harvey relies upon and trusts her readers to comprehend significance without excessive explication, drawing on an assumption of a shared centrality of emotion and memory in the human experience. The absence of detail serves to further highlight the universality of individual character's thoughts, emotions and feelings.

No dialogue Interior focus Implication over explication Stillness
Imagery

Anthropomorphising Earth

Anthropomorphising the Earth positions readers to view the planet as a vulnerable, responsive being whose fate is intertwined with that of humankind's. The astronauts 'watch it sleep and wake' (p. 3), and the planet is described as 'the face of an exulted lover' and 'a mother waiting for her children to return' (p. 3). Collapsing the distance between humanity and the environment reflects humanity's intimate connection with the Earth, reinforcing a moral imperative to care for the planet.

Metaphors draw continual connections between the manmade and natural worlds. A capsule's probe infiltrating the spaceship is compared to 'A bee entering a flower' (p. 78). The moon, too, is described as 'their silver companion' (p. 4), implying the living nature of all aspects of the cosmos, and hinting at the profound loneliness experienced by those aboard the spaceship.

Earth as character Extended metaphor Personification Moral imperative
Technique

Scale and Juxtaposition

Perhaps Harvey's most consistent and important technique is the juxtaposition of vastly different scales. A paragraph describing geological time — mountains forming over millions of years — is followed by a sentence about the smell of a food pouch. A passage of extraordinary cosmic beauty gives way to a description of routine maintenance. Chie's grief for her dying mother is held alongside the impersonal violence of a typhoon.

This juxtaposition is the novel's central argument. Harvey insists that both scales are real and both matter: individual human experience and geological time; private grief and planetary crisis; the mundane and the sublime. Neither cancels the other out. They coexist, uncomfortably and honestly, in the same orbiting moment.

Juxtaposition Scale Contrast Holding tension

Symbolism

Symbol

Las Meninas by Diego Velázquez (1599–1660)

To Shaun's wife the sixteenth century painting is 'an emblem of their first real exchange' (p. 7). On a symbolic level, the painting is suggestive of the layered dynamics of observation — the 'possibilities of subject and perspective' (p. 7) which permeate the text. Akin to chief court painter Velázquez's reframing of traditional royal portraiture — moving the focus from the monarch to other members of the royal household, including the titular ladies-in-waiting — Harvey's novel invites a reconsideration of the traditional view of planet Earth as significant and central within the wider universe.

Like the characters of Velázquez's painting, the crew are simultaneously observing and being observed. They watch the planet spinning below and literally and symbolically review their own past and current existence. The line between subject and object blurs, and we are prompted to ponder the separation between the viewer and the viewed.

Observation Subject & perspective Reframing
Symbol

Apollo 11's 1969 Photograph

The iconic photograph taken by Command Module Pilot Michael Collins on July 21, 1969, 384,400 kilometres from planet Earth, captures one of the first times humanity saw itself from a distance. Within the vast silence of space, the Earth appears small and alone but also whole and unified.

In many ways the iconic photograph provides a reflection of the space station, circling but never landing, distant from the planet, yet intimately connected to it. The crew's continued attempts to grapple with what it might mean to observe our own world from a distance are reflected in 'the infamous image … of the lunar module leaving the moon's surface, and of the earth beyond' (p. 42). However, while distance can facilitate an alternative perspective, Harvey warns that this does not necessarily ensure clarity.

Distance Perspective Humanity seeing itself
Symbol

The Mice

The ephemeral nature of existence is manifest in the 'forty resident mice' (p. 16). They are onboard 'enlightening them about muscle wastage in space' (p. 16), as much potential sources of scientific knowledge and understanding as the crew themselves. Within the microcosmic artificial habitat, the mice function to echo humanity's precarious existence and complete dependence on their environment.

Nell urges her rodent charges to 'Let go, be bold' (p. 54), reminding the reader that 'Life is short' (p. 54). The correlation between crew and mice is made explicit — 'watched by hundreds' (p. 85), and the mice are described as 'the lab rats who've made all things possible' (p. 94). There is a palpable sense of foreboding in Chie's warning to the mice: 'none of you get out of this alive. Not you small ones, not the big ones. You're all toast' (p. 50) — a levelling understanding of mortality that applies equally to the crew.

Mortality Parallel existence Dependence
Symbol

The Typhoon

The 'charging force' (p. 77) of the westward moving typhoon builds slowly and menacingly over the duration of much of the novel, coming to epitomise the consequences of humankind's failure to protect its natural environment. Determined eventually to be 'a super-typhoon' (p. 93), the storm's descent causes the momentary disappearance of Malaysia, Indonesia and the Philippines from the craft's view.

As a consequence of climate change and associated rising sea levels, less economically developed coastal regions are revealed as ill-equipped to withstand such events — pointing to the unjust reality that those least responsible for damaging emissions are often most exposed to its consequences. The suffering of the Filipino fisherman and his family who reside in 'a house made of tin, cardboard, hardboard and sticks' (p. 38) points to nature's indifference and the random victimisation of natural disasters.

From space, the violent storm is a phenomenon to observe and analyse. The astronauts recognise the typhoon as a powerfully resistant assertion, the Earth's demand for recognition — 'defiance, strength, vivacity, the bulge-eyed tongue-out warrior face worn in the haka' (p. 56). Their powerlessness in the face of the amassing storm reflects the feeling of helplessness and even guilt experienced by many as they witness the escalating impacts of climate change.

Climate crisis Inequality Nature's power Helplessness
Symbol

The Space Station

The space station in which the characters are trapped acts as a Foucauldian heterotopia — a space which is real but removed from ordinary existence. The 'nationless, borderless outpost' (p. 63) exists outside the domain of all national, cultural and territorial boundaries, contesting and at times inverting dominant norms of nationalism, traditional politics and Earth-bound priorities.

Harvey draws readers into a microcosm of contradictions which reflect the broader condition of Earth, both functioning as 'a complex life-support machine' (p. 19) in which even small changes can pose existential risks. The fragility of the spacecraft which 'could, instantaneously, all end – a failure in any part of the machine' (p. 19) reflects the precariousness of human civilisation and its dependence on an equally precarious ecological balance.

Concurrently, the space station also reminds us of all that is absent in our world — most significantly collaborative and peaceful pursuit of a single, collective mission.

Heterotopia Microcosm Fragility Cooperation

A Guide to Quoting Harvey

When selecting quotations for analytical writing, prioritise moments where Harvey's language is doing something that ordinary writing does not. Look for: shifts in narrative voice (especially to second person); moments where the rhythm of the sentence enacts what is being described; images that work across multiple levels simultaneously; and juxtapositions of scale or register that create meaning through contrast.

Avoid quoting long passages without analysis. A single well-chosen phrase, examined carefully, will always be more valuable than a paragraph of quotation with thin commentary. Ask of every quotation: what specific word or phrase is doing the work here, and why did Harvey choose it over alternatives?

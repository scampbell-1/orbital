Orbital  ·  Ideas, Concerns & Values

What the Novel Asks

Harvey does not provide answers. She creates a vantage point from which the questions become unavoidable. These five interconnected concerns drive the novel's intellectual and emotional work.

✦
Idea 1

Interconnectedness and Interdependence

The novel opens with a description of the six astronauts as 'so together … that even their thoughts, their internal mythologies, at times convene' (p. 1). Their cramped living conditions dissolve individual divisions to the point that 'Sometimes they dream the same dreams' (p. 1). Despite their global origins and geopolitical differences — manifested in the signage on the doors of the Russian and US 'national toilet' (p. 63) — the six arrive on the spacecraft with shared childhood experiences. They 'were all familiar with Winnie-the-Pooh in one form or another in their five different countries' (p. 47), and each experienced 'dreams of flying' (p. 70) as children.

The privileged orbital viewpoint illuminates the borderlessness of the planet — 'a rolling indivisible globe which knows no possibility of separation' (p. 72). While national differences are noted by crew members, they are tongue in cheek, allowing for moments of light-hearted connection. Chie chides that 'You American Christians get everywhere' (p. 15) and Shaun's wry response, 'Yep, you leave the surface of the earth and you still can't shake us' (p. 15), hints at the historic influence of their respective nations. Roman's gibe, 'We'll shake you soon' (p. 15), references the longstanding Cold War tensions between the United States and Russia.

They're companions, colleagues, mentors, doctors, dentists, hairdressers … they're each other's lifelines … They have to make do in lieu of every earthly thing

p. 18

The crew's need for connection extends to quiet moments of camaraderie — watching the Northern Lights together, 'a swathe of magenta that obscures the stars, and across the globe a shimmering hum of rolling light, of flickering, quavering, flooding light' (p. 41). Grief and loss are also revealed to be universal human experiences: Chie's mother's death leads her to go 'colourless with shock' (p. 8), while Nell experiences 'age-old grief for her long-dead mother' (p. 120). The crew 'out of necessity … come to see that their family is this one here' (p. 12).

Harvey also emphasises humanity's ecological interconnectedness. She describes humanity's current abnegation of care for its world as symptomatic of a metaphoric adolescent struggle, declaring the human species to be 'in the late smash-it-all-up teenage stage of self-harm and nihilism' (p. 30). However, the novel resists despondency, providing hope that humankind will eventually mature and accept its responsibility for ensuring the continuation of life on the planet.

Idea 2

Solitude and Insignificance

In a novel where 'Pick a single creature on this earth and its story will be the earth's story' (p. 107), personal insignificance takes on new meaning. Harvey challenges the dominance of anthropocentrism, contextualising human existence within the vastness and timelessness of the universe. Even the astronauts are reduced to 'the latest six of many' (p. 2), 'early warning systems' (p. 17), 'fortune tellers' (p. 23) and, ultimately, 'they are data' (p. 95).

The correlations between humanity and the animal kingdom are embodied in the mice aboard the ship. Distinctions are reduced to a matter of function — 'the only thing humans can do that other animals cannot is start fire from nothing' (p. 106). The metaphorical description of the crew as 'a seabird on a warm day drifting' (p. 11) acknowledges the similarities in mammalian experience, while Roman acknowledges feeling like 'an animal in a cage' (p. 101). Humankind is declared to be nothing more than a 'fair-to-middling upright ape' (p. 30).

The relative insignificance of humankind is strikingly illuminated in its juxtaposition against the sublime. The crew are placed in continual contrast to a cosmos which is predicted to continue for at least 'the next five hundred billion years' (p. 87). On occasion, the human world is reduced to 'a planet of normalish mass rotating about an average star in a solar system or average everything in a galaxy of innumerable many' (p. 29). Yet Harvey simultaneously presents the Earth as a wondrous, magical planet — a 'planet held hostage by humans' (p. 131).

When earth feels so far away that depression washes over them

p. 18

The confined characters echo the circumstances of many during COVID-19 lockdowns — enduring the loss of a loved one while unable to be present. Characters find solace in the trivialities of existence, with daily routines providing a grounding. The repetitive nature of life in the spacecraft manifests a muted endurance, a continual tethering to the solidity of the ship as well as the self, in a world where all consoling familiarities — Earth, loved ones, rituals — are beyond their reach.

Idea 3

Hope and Faith

Interstellar exploration is framed as 'a big-hearted fanciful spasm of hope' (p. 29) — evidence of humankind's ability to transcend limitations. The astronauts' disciplined exercise regimes, maintenance schedules, and even their communications with loved ones and strangers Roman engages with via radio, can be viewed as acts of quiet optimism. In the connections made and maintained while floating at such a great distance from humanity, the reader comes to recognise hope as a natural consequence of loss and isolation — 'in loneliness and curiosity and hope, humanity looks outward' (p. 29).

Harvey eschews a presentation of religion harnessed to dogma and doctrine. Instead, she presents faith as an often unspoken experience which threads through the lives of crew members — a spiritual register through which an attempt can be made to make sense of encounters with the vastness of space. Shaun is declared the soul, existing 'to convince them all that they have souls' (p. 19). In Nell's contemplation of the difference between her Atheism and Shaun's belief in 'a Creationist God' (p. 44), she comes to recognise belief as simply an alternative lens: 'Hers an occurrence of nature and his an artwork' (p. 45).

Pietro, too, recognises the possibility of the divine in the 'upper chamber that pours light on the seas' (p. 93), viewing the 'super-typhoon' (p. 93) from above — 'a godly view' (p. 73). Krikalev's photograph grows increasingly deified, 'more godly' (p. 134), until the historic cosmonaut speaks the world into existence: 'Let there be light, he seems quietly to say' (p. 134).

In the concluding moments of the text, a community shelters from the typhoon's flooding 'behind the altar of a chapel' (p. 134), a moment laden with spiritual symbolism as human and ecological vulnerability meet transcendent hope. 'The buffering of the trees has saved the chapel' (p. 134) — this grounded, Earth-bound image affirms the sacredness of the planet.

VATE Inside Stories

The novel calls attention to humanity's past and continuing threat to a wounded world in the name of progress — 'the atom bomb and … fake stars they're going to put into space in the shape of company logos, and the buildings they're going to print on the moon' (p. 51). However, the possibility of redemption is also recognised. Pietro's assertion that 'a person is not beautiful because they're good, they're beautiful because they're alive' (p. 51) reflects the resplendence of life, mere existence teeming with hopefulness.

Idea 4

Purpose and Meaning

The purpose of humanity's existence is a question at the forefront of Harvey's text. Like the spacecraft in which the six astronauts continuously orbit, 'A human being was not made to stand still' (p. 133). The novel provides non-judgemental commentary on humanity's endless quest for meaning — 'a desire for fulfilment that we can't quite scratch' (p. 30) — recognising the multitudinous pathways to a pursuit of purpose: technological achievements, national and personal duty, and even profoundly simple, human connection.

The astronauts recognise the arbitrary nature of time in the cosmos. Roman maintains a tally of his days in space, employing 'the arbitrary metric of time' (p. 7) in an attempt to tie his experience 'to something countable' (p. 5). The crew are advised to maintain a focus on 'Coordinated Universal Time' (p. 5) for timekeeping, recognising time as an artificial construct aimed at provisioning the capacity 'to anchor your mind' (p. 5).

Humanity's insatiable need 'for more, more of everything' (p. 26) is identified as motivation for even the six aboard the craft. Shaun contemplates whether 'man's lust for space is curiosity or ingratitude' (p. 49), pointing towards the tension between humanity's innate desire for discovery and a deeper discontentment. Pietro furthers this line of thinking, declaring 'these phallic ships thrust into space are surely the most hubristic … totems of a species gone mad with self-love' (p. 53).

Operating satellites, ex-satellites … flecks of paint, frozen engine coolant, the upper stages of rockets, bits of Sputnik 1 and Iridium 33 and Kosmos 2251, solid-rocket exhaust particles, a lost toolbag, a mislaid camera, a dropped pair of pliers and a pair of gloves.

pp. 130–131

The listing of detritus from past missions littering the cosmos points towards the carelessness of humankind, as the role of space exploration itself extends the impact of humanity into the orbital realm. Space is revealed to be 'just the new frontier' (p. 106) — a potential refuge from ecological collapse, but only for the elite. Descriptions of space as 'a virgin up-for-sale wilderness, this new black gold, this new domain ripe for the taking' (p. 131) present space as a site for continued imperialist ambitions.

The Cosmic Calendar

The chapter 'Orbit 13' pays homage to Carl Sagan's Cosmic Calendar — a method of compressing the universe's 13.7 billion years into a single calendar year. Harvey's introspective description of the 'cosmic clumping thumping clashing banging Wild West shoot-out of rock and gas and headlong combat of matter and gravity' (p. 111) lends the universe a chaotic birth. All of human civilisation is reduced to a fleeting moment — 'a brief light that flickers on and off again one single day in the middle of the year, remembered by nothing' (p. 114).

Idea 5

Fragility and Impermanence

Both the fragility of planet Earth and humanity's existential insignificance are highlighted by the spatial perspective Harvey provides. The vastness of space glimpsed through the 'observation dome' (p. 93) underscores the extent to which the crew are at the mercy of their physical environment, mirroring humanity's dependence upon a healthy planet. Away from their home planet, the astronauts' 'brains were hijacked and tumbling' (p. 79), and the 'atrophying of the body' (p. 80) catalysed by interstellar travel reflects the tenuousness of human life.

Nell's memories of the Challenger disaster, in which all seven astronauts perished — '… they were there and then they were gone, in seventy seconds. Gone.' (p. 48) — reinforce the precariousness of human existence. Chie's recollections of her mother's survival of the US bombing of Nagasaki (1945) reinforces the fine boundary between life and death. Like planet Earth, human life is rendered 'miraculously given' (p. 61).

The space station itself is a temporary and fragile home. The 'crack that's appeared' (p. 132) in its outer shell underscores concerns about the future of the vessel and the survival of the crew, while symbolically reflecting broader anxieties surrounding environmental instability. The spaceship is revealed to be tired, with 'hatches that get ever smaller and modules that get ever older' (p. 133). Its limited future is foretold — 'this craft will bow graciously out … [in] another thirty-five thousand orbits' (p. 135), and there is subsequent unease about the cessation of international cooperation symbolised by the endeavour.

We're caught in a universe of collision and drift, the long slow ripples of the first Big Bang as the cosmos breaks apart; the closest galaxies smash together, then those that are left scatter and flee one another until each is alone and there's only space, an expansion expanding into itself, an emptiness birthing itself, and in the cosmic calendar as it would exist then, all humans ever did and were will be a brief light that flickers on and off again one single day in the middle of the year, remembered by nothing.

p. 114

Harvey's perspective throughout suggests that like the beginnings of the universe, the human species experiences a volatile state of flux — a collection of unresolvable conflicts, including the need for solitude and yearning for connection, a natural inclination towards conflict, and a cherishing of unity. Any sense of clarity or coherence — spiritual or emotional — is revealed as highly ephemeral. The novel offers no solid epiphanies. Rather, it invites acknowledgement of the relentless movement and change to which our universe, our planet and our species is subject.

How Ideas Interconnect

The strength of Orbital as a VCE text lies in how Harvey weaves these ideas together rather than presenting them as separate concerns. Interconnectedness and solitude are two sides of the same experience. Hope exists precisely because fragility exists. The quest for purpose is shadowed by the recognition of insignificance. Harvey implies that we are constantly hurled between isolation and intimacy, with human connection shown to be fragile, temporary, and subject to powerful and uncontrollable forces.

When writing analytically, the most sophisticated approach is to identify the tensions between ideas — the places where Harvey refuses to resolve a contradiction — and analyse how she uses language, structure, and perspective to hold those tensions in place.

Essay Angle — Ideas, Concerns & Values

Avoid writing about ideas in isolation. The most effective analytical responses to Orbital will show how Harvey brings multiple ideas into dialogue with one another — and how her language choices support, complicate, or deepen the thematic work the novel is doing. Ask: what does Harvey want the reader to feel about this idea? How does her prose achieve that effect? Where does she refuse to resolve a tension, and why does that matter?
